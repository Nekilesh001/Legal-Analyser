# NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement) — Mandatory Clauses Under Indian Law

## Clause: Clear Definition of Confidential Information
Act: Indian Contract Act 1872; Information Technology Act 2000
Contract type: nda
Requirement: An NDA must precisely define what constitutes "Confidential Information." Overly broad definitions may be challenged; overly narrow ones create gaps. The definition should cover: business plans, financial data, customer lists, technical specifications, trade secrets, and any information marked "Confidential."
Standard counter-clause: "'Confidential Information' means any non-public information, whether oral, written, or in any other medium, that is designated as confidential or that a reasonable person would understand to be confidential given the nature of the information and circumstances of disclosure. This includes but is not limited to: trade secrets, business strategies, pricing, customer data, technical specifications, and financial information."

## Clause: Specific Exclusions from Confidential Information
Act: Indian Contract Act 1872, Section 10 (certainty of terms)
Contract type: nda
Requirement: Without clear exclusions, disputes arise about what information is protected. Standard exclusions must be included: information already in public domain (without breach), independently developed, or disclosed with consent.
Standard counter-clause: "Confidential Information shall not include information that: (a) is or becomes publicly available other than through breach of this agreement; (b) was rightfully known to the receiving party before disclosure without restriction; (c) is independently developed by the receiving party without reference to the Confidential Information; or (d) is required to be disclosed by law, court order, or regulatory authority (with prompt notice given to the disclosing party)."

## Clause: Compelled Disclosure — Government/Court Order
Act: Right to Information Act 2005; CrPC Sections 91–92; SEBI / RBI regulatory requirements
Contract type: nda
Requirement: When legally compelled to disclose (court order, regulatory demand, RTI), the receiving party must disclose but should first notify the disclosing party to allow them to seek a protective order. Failure to include this clause leaves the disclosing party without recourse.
Standard counter-clause: "If the Receiving Party is required by law, regulation, or court order to disclose Confidential Information, it shall: (a) promptly notify the Disclosing Party before disclosure (to the extent permitted by law); (b) cooperate with the Disclosing Party in seeking a protective order or confidential treatment; and (c) disclose only the minimum amount of Confidential Information required."

## Clause: Purpose Limitation
Act: IT (Reasonable Security Practices) Rules 2011; Indian Contract Act 1872
Contract type: nda
Requirement: Confidential Information should only be used for the specific purpose for which it was shared (e.g., evaluating a potential business relationship). Use for any other purpose, even if not directly disclosed to third parties, constitutes a breach.
Standard counter-clause: "The Receiving Party shall use the Confidential Information solely for the purpose of [specifically defined purpose, e.g., evaluating a potential business transaction between the parties] ('Permitted Purpose') and for no other purpose whatsoever without the prior written consent of the Disclosing Party."

## Clause: Need-to-Know Access Controls
Act: IT (Reasonable Security Practices) Rules 2011; general data protection principles
Contract type: nda
Requirement: The receiving party must restrict internal access to confidential information. Allowing all employees unrestricted access to a competitor's confidential data is negligent and may result in inadvertent disclosure or misuse.
Standard counter-clause: "The Receiving Party shall disclose Confidential Information only to its employees, directors, advisors, and contractors ('Representatives') who have a genuine need to know for the Permitted Purpose, and only after informing such Representatives of the confidentiality obligations. The Receiving Party shall be liable for any breach of this agreement by its Representatives."

## Clause: Data Security Standards for Electronic Information
Act: Information Technology Act 2000, Section 43A; IT (Reasonable Security Practices) Rules 2011
Contract type: nda
Requirement: For NDAs involving electronic data or sensitive personal data, the receiving party must implement and maintain reasonable security practices as defined by the IT Rules. This includes encryption of data in transit and at rest, access controls, and breach notification procedures.
Standard counter-clause: "The Receiving Party shall implement and maintain reasonable security measures to protect Confidential Information from unauthorized access, disclosure, or destruction. For digital Confidential Information, such measures shall include: encryption of data in transit and at rest using industry-standard protocols, role-based access controls, and regular security assessments."

## Clause: Duration of Confidentiality Obligation
Act: Indian Contract Act 1872; trade secret protection principles
Contract type: nda
Requirement: The confidentiality obligation must have a defined duration. Perpetual obligations are generally enforceable only for true trade secrets. For regular business information, 2–5 years post-termination is standard and enforceable in India. The NDA must specify this duration.
Standard counter-clause: "The Receiving Party's obligations under this agreement shall continue during the term of the agreement and for a period of [two/three/five] years following its termination or expiration. For information that constitutes a trade secret under applicable law, obligations shall continue until such information ceases to be a trade secret through no fault of the Receiving Party."

## Clause: Return or Destruction of Confidential Information
Act: Indian Contract Act 1872; IT Act 2000, Section 72
Contract type: nda
Requirement: Upon termination or request, all confidential information (and copies) must be returned or certified as destroyed. Retaining confidential information after termination constitutes a continuing breach. Electronic copies must specifically be addressed.
Standard counter-clause: "Upon termination of this agreement or upon written request by the Disclosing Party, the Receiving Party shall promptly: (a) return all tangible Confidential Information; (b) permanently delete all electronic copies from its systems (including backups) and certify such deletion in writing within ten business days; and (c) destroy any notes, analyses, or summaries prepared based on the Confidential Information."

## Clause: No License or IP Transfer
Act: Copyright Act 1957; Patents Act 1970; Indian Contract Act 1872
Contract type: nda
Requirement: An NDA must be explicit that sharing confidential information does not grant any license, right, or transfer of intellectual property. Without this, a receiving party may claim an implied license to use the shared technology or content.
Standard counter-clause: "Nothing in this agreement shall be construed to grant the Receiving Party any right, title, interest, or license in or to any Confidential Information or any intellectual property rights of the Disclosing Party. The Disclosing Party retains all proprietary rights in the Confidential Information."

## Clause: No Solicitation of Employees
Act: Indian Contract Act 1872; Section 27 (restraint of trade jurisprudence)
Contract type: nda
Requirement: NDAs executed in the context of business evaluations often include non-solicitation of employees provisions to prevent a prospective partner from poaching key staff after accessing the disclosing party's team structure. A reasonable (12–24 month) non-solicitation of employees clause is enforceable in India.
Standard counter-clause: "During the term of this agreement and for a period of twelve months thereafter, neither party shall, directly or indirectly, solicit or hire any employee of the other party who was introduced to or interacted with in connection with the Permitted Purpose, without prior written consent of the other party. General recruitment advertisements open to the public are excluded from this restriction."

## Clause: Governing Law and Jurisdiction
Act: Indian Contract Act 1872; Civil Procedure Code 1908
Contract type: nda
Requirement: The NDA must specify the applicable law and jurisdiction. For NDAs between Indian parties, Indian law and a specific Indian court jurisdiction is standard. For cross-border NDAs, conflicts of law issues must be addressed.
Standard counter-clause: "This agreement shall be governed by the laws of India. Any disputes arising from this agreement shall be subject to the exclusive jurisdiction of the courts in [city]. The parties consent to service of process through their registered addresses."

## Clause: Injunctive Relief for Breach
Act: Specific Relief Act 1963, Section 38; Civil Procedure Code 1908, Order 39
Contract type: nda
Requirement: Monetary damages are often inadequate for confidentiality breaches. The NDA should expressly recognize the right to seek interim injunctive relief from courts without proving actual monetary harm, preventing ongoing or threatened disclosures.
Standard counter-clause: "The parties acknowledge that a breach of this agreement would cause irreparable harm for which monetary damages would be an inadequate remedy. The Disclosing Party shall be entitled to seek interim and permanent injunctive relief in a court of competent jurisdiction without the requirement to post security or bond, in addition to all other available remedies."

## Clause: Mutual vs. One-Sided Obligations
Act: Indian Contract Act 1872 (fairness and consideration principles)
Contract type: nda
Requirement: A one-sided NDA that only binds one party but allows the other to share information freely is inequitable and may create unintended commercial leverage. In any genuine bilateral business evaluation, a mutual NDA is appropriate and expected.
Standard counter-clause: "This agreement creates mutual obligations: each party shall protect the other party's Confidential Information with the same degree of care (but no less than reasonable care) that it uses to protect its own most sensitive proprietary information."

## Clause: Residuals Clause (Dangerous if Unrestricted)
Act: Indian Contract Act 1872; intellectual property principles
Contract type: nda
Requirement: A "residuals clause" that allows the receiving party to retain and use information remembered in unaided human memory (not written notes or systems) is extremely dangerous to include in a technology NDA and can effectively nullify the confidentiality protection for all verbally disclosed information.
Standard counter-clause: "[NOTE: The residuals clause proposed by the receiving party has been removed. Such clauses effectively allow the receiving party's employees to use any verbally disclosed confidential information in subsequent projects without restriction, creating an unacceptable risk. If the receiving party insists, the clause must be limited to general skills and knowledge only and must explicitly exclude technical specifications, trade secrets, and customer data.]"

## Clause: Notice Period for Breach Claims
Act: Limitation Act 1963; Indian Contract Act 1872
Contract type: nda
Requirement: The NDA should specify a procedure for notifying the other party of a suspected breach, allowing for cure before litigation. A direct claim without any cure opportunity may escalate minor incidents unnecessarily.
Standard counter-clause: "Upon discovering a breach or suspected breach of this agreement, the affected party shall notify the other party in writing within ten business days of discovery, describing the nature of the breach. The breaching party shall have ten business days to cure the breach (where it is capable of cure) before the affected party may seek formal remedies."

## Clause: Remedies for Unauthorized Disclosure
Act: Indian Contract Act 1872, Section 74; IT Act 2000, Section 72; Specific Relief Act 1963
Contract type: nda
Requirement: The NDA should provide for specific remedies including both injunctive relief and damages. A clause limiting remedies only to monetary damages is insufficient for confidentiality breaches where ongoing disclosure causes irreparable harm.
Standard counter-clause: "In the event of actual or threatened breach of this agreement, the Disclosing Party shall be entitled to: (a) injunctive relief to prevent further disclosure; (b) recovery of all damages sustained; (c) recovery of the Disclosing Party's legal costs and expenses; and (d) any other remedy available under applicable law. The Receiving Party acknowledges that breach may cause harm beyond what is compensable in damages alone."
